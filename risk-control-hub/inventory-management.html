Inventory Management

Simple controls to track, count, protect, and understand the products your business owns.

Start with basic controls View common risks
Plain English Overview

What is Inventory Management?

Inventory management is how your business keeps track of the products it buys, stores, sells, uses, damages, returns, or removes from stock.

You do not need an expensive system to start. You need a basic way to know what came in, what went out, what is still available, and whether your records match what is physically there.

This process helps you answer:

  • What products do we have?
  • Where are those products located?
  • What was received, sold, used, or removed?
  • Are damaged or expired items identified?
  • Do records match the physical inventory?
Why this matters

Inventory problems can quietly reduce profit.

Missing, damaged, expired, or poorly tracked products often become visible too late. A simple inventory routine helps you protect products and make better business decisions.

Protect your products

Basic controls help reduce the chance of products being lost, stolen, misplaced, or forgotten.

Reduce losses

Regular counts and reviews help identify missing, damaged, or expired products earlier.

Avoid stock surprises

Better inventory visibility helps you avoid selling products you do not actually have.

Improve purchasing decisions

Knowing what is really available helps you decide what to buy, when to buy it, and how much to buy.

What can go wrong?

Common inventory management issues

In audit language, these are inventory risks. In business language, these are situations that can lead to lost money, bad decisions, customer issues, or products disappearing without explanation.

01

Products are lost, stolen, or misplaced.

02

Inventory records do not match physical stock.

03

Products are received but not documented.

04

Products are sold, used, or removed without updating records.

05

Damaged or expired products are not identified.

06

Purchases are made without knowing real stock levels.

Minimum Controls

Minimum controls you can start using now

These controls are simple habits that help you understand what inventory you have, what changed, and what needs attention.

Keep a basic inventory list

Track your main products using a simple spreadsheet, notebook, POS report, inventory app, or system.

Document products received

Record what arrived, when it arrived, who received it, and what support exists.

Document products sold, used, or removed

Inventory should be updated when products leave the business, are used internally, or are removed from stock.

Count inventory regularly

Compare physical inventory against your records on a regular schedule.

Review damaged or expired items

Identify products that cannot be sold or used and document what action was taken.

Limit who can adjust inventory records

Only the right people should be able to change inventory quantities, locations, or adjustments.

Simple Risk & Control Cards

Turn inventory risks into practical controls.

Each card explains what can go wrong, why it matters, what basic control can help, what evidence to keep, and how often to do it.

Risk 01

Inventory records do not match physical stock

Why it matters:

The business may think it has products available when it does not, or may fail to notice missing, damaged, or misplaced items.

Basic control:

Perform regular inventory counts and compare the physical count with the inventory record.

Evidence to keep:

Inventory count sheet, inventory list, adjustment notes, photos if needed, and review notes.

Frequency: Weekly, monthly, or based on sales volume Owner: Owner, store manager, or operations lead
Risk 02

Products are received but not documented

Why it matters:

The business may pay for products that were not received, lose visibility over stock, or fail to update available inventory.

Basic control:

Document products received and compare them against the purchase order, invoice, receipt, or supplier confirmation.

Evidence to keep:

Receiving log, supplier invoice, delivery note, purchase order, photos, or system record.

Frequency: Every delivery or inventory receipt Owner: Person receiving inventory, reviewed when possible
Risk 03

Damaged, expired, or missing items are not reviewed

Why it matters:

The business may keep unusable products in records, sell poor-quality items, lose money, or make purchasing decisions based on inaccurate inventory.

Basic control:

Review damaged, expired, slow-moving, or missing products and document what action was taken.

Evidence to keep:

Damage log, expiration review, adjustment record, photos, disposal record, or approval note.

Frequency: Monthly or during each inventory count Owner: Owner, store manager, warehouse lead, or operations lead
Evidence Examples

What evidence should you keep?

Evidence is simply proof that the control happened. It does not need to be complicated. It needs to be clear, saved, and easy to find later.

Inventory list

An inventory list is a basic record of the products the business has. It can include product name, quantity, location, cost, selling price, and status.

Count sheet

A count sheet is used to write down the quantity physically counted for each product during an inventory count.

Receiving log

A receiving log documents products received from a supplier, including date, quantity, condition, and who received them.

Supplier invoice

A supplier invoice shows what the supplier charged the business for products, including quantities, prices, dates, and payment details.

Delivery note

A delivery note is a document that shows what was delivered. It helps confirm whether the products received match what was expected.

Adjustment record

An adjustment record explains why inventory quantities were changed, such as damage, loss, correction, count difference, or internal use.

Damage or expiration log

A damage or expiration log records products that cannot be sold or used because they are damaged, expired, spoiled, or otherwise unusable.

Photos of inventory condition

Photos can help support the condition of products, especially when documenting damage, expiration, missing items, or disposal.

Implementation Roadmap

How to start this week

Start with a simple routine. The goal is not to build a perfect inventory system in one day. The goal is to know what came in, what went out, and what is really there.

Step 1

List your main products

Start with the products that matter most to the business: high-value items, fast-moving items, or products that are easy to lose.

Step 2

Choose where inventory will be tracked

Use a spreadsheet, notebook, POS report, inventory app, or system. The tool can be simple, but it must be consistent.

Step 3

Document every product received

Record what arrived, when it arrived, who received it, and what document supports it.

Step 4

Document every product sold, used, or removed

Inventory should change when products leave the business, are used internally, damaged, expired, returned, or adjusted.

Step 5

Count a small group of products regularly

You do not need to count everything every day. Start with the most important products and create a realistic count routine.

Step 6

Investigate differences between records and physical stock

If the count does not match the records, document the difference, possible reason, and action taken.

Related Concepts

Terms connected to this process

These concepts will be explained in the Audit Glossary in plain English. For now, click each concept to see a simple definition.

Inventory Count

An inventory count is the process of physically counting products and comparing the count to the inventory records.

Receiving

Receiving is the step where the business confirms that products from a supplier actually arrived and were accepted.

Inventory Adjustment

An inventory adjustment is a change made to inventory records to explain differences, damage, loss, corrections, or internal use.

Shrinkage

Shrinkage is inventory that is missing, lost, stolen, damaged, or otherwise unavailable compared to what the records say should exist.

Obsolete Inventory

Obsolete inventory is stock that is no longer useful, sellable, needed, or current because it is outdated, expired, damaged, or no longer in demand.

Control

A control is a step, review, approval, limit, or routine that helps reduce the chance of something going wrong in the business.

Evidence

Evidence is proof that something happened. In inventory, it can show that products were counted, received, adjusted, reviewed, or removed properly.

Control Owner

A control owner is the person responsible for performing, reviewing, or making sure a control happens on time.
