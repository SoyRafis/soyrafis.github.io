Cash Management

Simple controls to understand, protect, and review how money moves through your business.

Start with basic controls View common risks
Plain English Overview

What is Cash Management?

Cash management is how your business keeps track of money coming in, money going out, bank activity, payments, receipts, and available cash.

You do not need a complex system to start. You need a basic routine that helps you know where the money went, who approved it, what support exists, and whether the bank balance makes sense.

This process helps you answer:

  • How much money does the business really have?
  • Who can move money out of the business?
  • Are payments reviewed before they are made?
  • Is there support for expenses and transfers?
  • Is bank activity reviewed regularly?
Why this matters

Cash problems can hide inside daily activity.

Many small businesses do not lose control because of one big mistake. They lose control because small cash issues repeat over time without review.

Protect your money

Basic reviews help reduce the chance of unauthorized or unsupported payments.

Improve visibility

Regular bank reviews help you understand what is happening with cash.

Catch unusual activity

Unusual payments, transfers, or missing support become easier to spot.

Make better decisions

Better cash visibility supports better spending, planning, and business decisions.

What can go wrong?

Common cash management issues

In audit language, these are risks. In business language, these are situations that can cost you money, create confusion, or hide problems.

01

Payments are made without review or approval.

02

Business and personal expenses get mixed.

03

Bank activity is not checked regularly.

04

Receipts, invoices, or payment support are missing.

05

The owner does not know the real cash position.

06

Unusual transactions are ignored or explained too late.

Minimum Controls

Minimum controls you can start using now

These controls are not about bureaucracy. They are simple habits that help you protect cash and create visibility.

Review bank activity weekly

Check deposits, payments, transfers, fees, and unusual activity at least once a week.

Keep support for every payment

Save the invoice, receipt, approval, payment confirmation, or reason for every payment.

Separate preparation and approval when possible

The person preparing a payment should not be the only person approving it.

Reconcile bank accounts monthly

Compare bank statements against your records to confirm that the balance makes sense.

Limit who can move money

Keep bank access, payment apps, cards, and transfer permissions limited to the right people.

Document unusual transactions

If something looks unusual, write down what happened, why it happened, and who reviewed it.

Simple Risk & Control Cards

Turn cash risks into practical controls.

Each card explains what can go wrong, why it matters, what basic control can help, what evidence to keep, and how often to do it.

Risk 01

Payments are made without review

Why it matters:

Money can leave the business without proper approval, support, or business purpose.

Basic control:

Someone should review and approve payments before they are released.

Evidence to keep:

Invoice, receipt, approval message, payment confirmation, and bank record.

Frequency: Every payment Owner: Business owner or finance lead
Risk 02

Bank activity is not reviewed

Why it matters:

Errors, unexpected charges, duplicate payments, or suspicious activity may go unnoticed.

Basic control:

Review bank activity on a regular schedule and investigate anything unusual.

Evidence to keep:

Bank statement, review notes, screenshots, reconciliation file, or marked transactions.

Frequency: Weekly or monthly Owner: Owner, manager, or finance lead
Risk 03

Business and personal expenses are mixed

Why it matters:

The business may lose visibility over true expenses, cash position, taxes, and financial performance.

Basic control:

Use separate business accounts and document any owner-related payments or reimbursements.

Evidence to keep:

Receipts, reimbursement records, owner draw notes, bank records, and accounting entries.

Frequency: Ongoing Owner: Business owner
Evidence Examples

What evidence should you keep?

Evidence is simply proof that the control happened. It does not need to be complicated. It needs to be clear, saved, and easy to find later.

Bank statements

A bank statement is a document from the bank that shows money coming in, money going out, fees, transfers, and the account balance for a specific period.

Invoices

An invoice explains what was purchased or sold, who charged it, how much it cost, and when payment is expected or was requested.

Receipts

A receipt is proof that a payment happened. It usually shows the date, amount, vendor, payment method, and what was purchased.

Payment confirmations

A payment confirmation is proof that money was actually sent. It can come from a bank, payment app, credit card platform, or online banking system.

Approval emails or messages

An approval message shows that someone reviewed and approved a payment, transfer, or expense before or after it happened, depending on the control.

Bank reconciliation file

A bank reconciliation file is an Excel spreadsheet used to match your business records against your bank statement. It helps you make sure the money in the bank aligns with what you think you have, and lets you note down the reasons for any differences (like a check that hasn't cleared yet).

Cash count sheets

A cash count sheet is a simple record used to count physical cash, compare it to expected cash, and document any difference.

Expense reports

An expense report summarizes business expenses, explains why they happened, and includes support like receipts, invoices, or approvals.

Implementation Roadmap

How to start this week

Start small. The goal is not to create a perfect system in one day. The goal is to create a routine you can actually maintain.

Step 1

List all bank accounts and payment methods

Include bank accounts, credit cards, payment apps, online banking access, and cash boxes if applicable.

Step 2

Identify who can move money

Write down who can prepare payments, approve payments, make transfers, or use business cards.

Step 3

Start saving support for every payment

Every payment should have a reason, support, and confirmation saved somewhere consistent.

Step 4

Review bank activity weekly

Look for unexpected charges, duplicate payments, missing deposits, transfers, or unusual activity.

Step 5

Reconcile bank accounts monthly

Compare your bank statement with your records and investigate anything that does not make sense.

Step 6

Investigate unusual transactions

Do not rely on memory. Add a note explaining what happened, who reviewed it, and what support exists.

Related Concepts

Terms connected to this process

These concepts will be explained in the Audit Glossary in plain English. For now, click each concept to see a simple definition.

Control

A control is a step, review, approval, limit, or routine that helps reduce the chance of something going wrong in the business.

Evidence

Evidence is proof that something happened. In controls, it helps show that a review, approval, reconciliation, or check was actually performed.

Preventive Control

A preventive control is designed to stop a problem before it happens. For example, requiring approval before a payment is released.

Detective Control

A detective control helps identify a problem after it happened. For example, reviewing bank activity to find unusual or unsupported transactions.

Segregation of Duties

Segregation of duties means not giving one person full control over an entire process. For example, the person preparing a payment should not be the only person approving it.

Bank Reconciliation

A bank reconciliation compares the bank statement with the business records to confirm that the cash balance makes sense and any differences are explained.

Control Owner

A control owner is the person responsible for performing, reviewing, or making sure a control happens on time.
